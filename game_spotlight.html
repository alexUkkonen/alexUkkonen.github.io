Outerwilds

Outerwilds is one of the best examples of utilizing the singleplayer aspect of the game in game de-sign. In the game you are stuck in a time loop that lasts ca 20 minutes. The time loop always ends with the sun exploding and destroying the solar system. Trough the loops you gather information and try to find a way to save everyone. This makes the whole game a puzzle. The game has a lot of unique planets with new mechanics and puzzles and everywhere you find clues left by aliens eons ago.
While all of this is impressive in its own right the most impressive thing is the feeling of gravity that the planets exude. This impressive simulation of gravity is not unique, but it is extremely rare and what makes it so interesting is how they did it. So, what is the solution? It’s deceptively simple. The player never moves, instead the world moves around the player. To you it looks like you are landing on a planet, but in reality, the planet is landing on you.
The game effectively uses the fact that even in the real-world velocity is relative. When you walk backwards in a train you feel like you are moving in that direction, but compared to the earth you are still moving in the opposite direction. That is because you perceive your direction of movement relative to the train.
